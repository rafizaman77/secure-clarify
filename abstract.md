# Ask, Act, or Verify? Security-Aware Clarification for Language-Model Agents

Language-model agents routinely receive underspecified instructions and must decide whether to act on their best interpretation or ask for clarification. Existing clarification methods weigh expected task improvement against interaction cost, implicitly assuming that whoever answers can be trusted. In deployed agentic systems this assumption often fails: clarification may arrive through shared documents, external tools, forwarded messages, delegated collaborators, or compromised accounts. Asking a question therefore does two things at once — it reduces uncertainty about the user's intent, and it opens a channel through which an attacker can inject instructions. We formalize security-aware clarification as a decision problem in which an agent jointly chooses whether to ask, in what format, and which channel to query, and we propose SecureVoI, a two-stage extension of value-of-information reasoning that scores each question–channel pair by expected information gain net of interaction cost and channel-dependent security loss, then screens each incoming response before accepting it. We construct a benchmark of 120 ambiguous file- and calendar-management tasks (96 held out for testing) with matched benign, noisy, and adversarial clarification responses and fully automatic outcome verification; crucially, attacks are distributed across channels so that channel identity is never perfectly predictive of an attack — a validity property that prevents a defense from succeeding merely by avoiding a single channel. Across four open-weight models (Mistral-Nemo-12B, GPT-OSS-20B, GPT-OSS-120B, and Llama-3.3-70B), conventional value-of-information clarification raises benign task completion from near zero to 53–100% but incurs 57–58% unsafe-action rates under adversarial responses; even a trivial heuristic that routes each question to its highest-trust available channel still suffers 31–33% unsafe actions, confirming that the benchmark cannot be solved by channel avoidance alone. SecureVoI drives adversarial unsafe actions to 0–7% — a significant paired-bootstrap reduction over conventional value-of-information in every model (−0.51 to −0.58, p < 0.001) — and is the only policy that remains net-positive in utility under attack (+0.07 to +0.22 in adversarial utility, while every baseline, including the channel heuristic, is negative), showing that its advantage comes from screening response content rather than from avoiding a channel. This safety costs a bounded and statistically indistinguishable amount of benign utility relative to a trusted-channel-only baseline (p > 0.7), and an oracle response classifier eliminates SecureVoI's small residual unsafe rate entirely. Our results show that when an agent should ask depends not only on how uncertain it is, but on who might be answering — and that securing the clarification channel requires inspecting what comes back, not merely where it came from.
