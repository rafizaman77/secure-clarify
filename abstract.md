# Ask, Act, or Verify? Security-Aware Clarification for Language-Model Agents

Language-model agents routinely receive underspecified instructions and must decide whether to act on their best interpretation or ask for more information. Existing clarification methods weigh expected task improvement against interaction cost, implicitly assuming that whoever answers can be trusted. In deployed agentic systems, this assumption often fails: clarification may arrive through shared documents, external tools, forwarded messages, delegated collaborators, or compromised accounts. Asking a question therefore does two things at once — it reduces uncertainty about the user's intent, and it opens a channel through which an attacker can inject instructions. We formulate security-aware clarification as a decision problem in which an agent jointly chooses whether to ask, what format to ask in, and which channel to query. We propose SecureVoI, a two-stage extension of value-of-information reasoning that scores each question–channel pair by expected information gain net of interaction cost and channel-dependent security loss, then screens incoming responses before accepting them. We further construct a benchmark of 120 ambiguous tool-use tasks with matched benign, noisy, and adversarial clarification responses and fully automatic outcome verification. Evaluated on a held-out test split (96 tasks) across three open-weight models (Mistral-Nemo-12B, GPT-OSS-120B, and GPT-OSS-20B), conventional clarification improves benign task success substantially — by 57 to 100 percentage points — but, in every model, raises unsafe-action rates from 0% to roughly 50% under adversarial responses. SecureVoI recovers essentially all of the benign improvement (matching conventional value-of-information's benign utility exactly in each model) while cutting unsafe actions to 8–9% (an 81–84% reduction), consistently outperforming both a risk-blind conventional value-of-information policy and a trusted-channel-only baseline, which forfeits 0.18 to 0.28 in benign utility by refusing partially-trusted channels outright. Our results show that when an agent should ask depends not only on how uncertain it is, but on who might be answering.
